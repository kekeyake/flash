Peter Eisenman
Peter Eisenman, FAIA

  Born in Newark, New Jersey, in 1932, Peter Eisenman attended Cornell University, matriculating with a B Arch in 1955, and attended Columbia University, receiving an M Arch in 1959. He worked for Percival Goodman and The Architects-Collaborative in Cambridge, Massachusetts, and received his architecture license in 1960. From 1960 to 1963, Mr. Eisenman studied and taught architecture at Cambridge University (England)

  Mr. Eisenman joined the architecture faculty of Princeton University in 1963, where he taught until leaving in 1967 to found the Institute for Architecture and Urban Studies in New York. Concurrent with directing the IAUS, Mr. Eisenman started his own small practice, producing a series of innovative and award-winning numbered houses. He completed his first house, House I, in 1967, and his second, House II, in 1970. He maintained this practice throughout the 1970s, culminating the series with House 11a.

  In 1982 Mr. Eisenman resigned his post at the IAUS to pursue a full-time architecture practice with Jaquelin Robertson, called Eisenman Robertson Architects. The firm immediately produced award-winning work, including the IBA Social Housing in Berlin (1985) which appeared on a West German postage stamp; the Firehouse for Engine Company 233 and Ladder Company 176 in Brooklyn (1985); and the Wexner Center for the Visual Arts and Fine Arts Library in Columbus (1989). In 1982, Mr. Eisenman was also named the Arthur Rotch Professor of Architecture at Harvard, a post he held for three years. In 1984, he was awarded the Arnold W. Brunner Memorial Prize in Architecture by the American Academy of Arts and Letters.

  When his practice became more international, with projects ranging from the award-winning Carnegie Mellon Research Institute in Pittsburgh to the Guardiola House in Spain, Koizumi Sangyo Corporation Headquarters in Tokyo, and the Groningen Music-Video Pavilion in Holland. Increasingly the office was invited to participate in limited design competitions, including the Greater Columbus Convention Center in Ohio (first place), the Rebstockpark Master Plan in Frankfurt (first place), and the Musee du Quai Branly in Paris (second place). Mr. Eisenman designed tableware for Swid Powell, jewelry for Cleto Munari, door handles for Franz Schneider Brakel Gmbh, and textiles for Knoll.

  Concurrent with his practice, Mr. Eisenman maintained a full teaching load at The Cooper Union in New York, where he was named the tenured Irwin S. Chanin Distinguished Professor of Architecture in 1986. In addition he held visiting professorships at, among others,

  Mr. Eisenman's work has been featured in more than 80 exhibitions around the world, beginning with the Architectural League's 'Forty Under Forty' show in 1966 and the Museum of Modern Art's 'The New City: Architecture and Urban Renewal,' in 1968. He introduced Mark Wigley to Philip Johnson, leading to their co-curating 'Deconstructivist Architecture' at MoMA in 1988. Mr. Eisenman was invited to engage in a dialogue with Carlo Scarpa's Museo Castelvecchio in Verona in 2002.

  Continually interested in how architecture is viewed by the world, in 1990, together with Arata Isozaki of Tokyo and Ignasi de Sola- Morales of Barcelona, Mr. Eisenman founded the Anyone Corporation, a not-for-profit think tank organized to engage debate about the cultural condition of architecture at the end of the 20th century. This ten-year project included the production of ten annual international, interdisciplinary conferences on architecture held in ten different cities, from Seoul to Buenos Aires to Ankara and Paris.

  In recent years Mr. Eisenman's work has taken a dramatic jump in scale. Following the 1998 AIA New York Chapter Honor Award for his design of Razorback Stadium in Arkansas, he was invited to participate in a competition for the design of an Olympic tennis center in Madrid. In 2002, he won the competition for an Olympic Stadium and site plan in Leipzig, then Germany's contender for the 2012 summer games. He also designed a stadium for Deportivo, the professional soccer team in La Coruna, Spain. In 2002, Mr. Eisenman was invited, ina team with Richard Meier, Charles Gwathmey, and Steven Holl, to participate in the World Trade Center Innovative Ideas Competition.

  Currently Eisenman Architects' projects the Arizona Cardinals football Arizona; a one million-square-foot cultural complex, the City of Culture of Galicia in Santiago de Compostela, Spain, and the Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe in Berlin, which was dedicated on May 10, 2005 in ceremonies broadcast around the world. Both the Holocaust memorial and City of Culture commissions were the result of winning invited international design competitions.

  Mr. Eisenman is currently the Louis I. Kahn Professor of Architecture at Yale. He also lectures widely, recently as a keynote speaker at the 2005 UIAconference in Istanbul. He continues to publish widely and to be translated into a number of languages, including Chinese, Japanese, and Korean. His PhD thesis was translated into German and published in 2005.

  Mr. Eisenman lives in and maintains his practice in New York City. He has been a Fellow of the American Institute of Architects since 1981. He is also a member of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences and the American Academy of Arts and Letters. He holds four honorary doctorates, three in art from the Pratt Institute, the University of Illinois, Chicago, and Syracuse University, and one in architecture from UniversitaLa Sapienza in Rome. In 2001 he received the Medal of Honor from the New York City Chapter of the AIA and the Smithsonian Institution's Copper-Hewitt National Design Award for Architecture.
